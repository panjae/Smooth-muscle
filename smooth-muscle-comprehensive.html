I. OVERVIEW

Smooth muscle is found in the walls of hollow organs and blood vessels throughout the body. Unlike skeletal muscle, smooth muscle lacks striations and is under involuntary control. It plays crucial roles in regulating blood pressure, moving food through the digestive tract, and controlling airway diameter.

Key Characteristics

  • Involuntary control: Regulated by autonomic nervous system
  • No striations: Lacks the regular arrangement of sarcomeres
  • Single nucleus: Each cell contains one centrally located nucleus
  • Gap junctions: Allow electrical and chemical coupling between cells
  • Slow, sustained contractions: Can maintain tension for long periods
  • Length adaptation: Can function over a wide range of lengths

Location and Function

Smooth muscle is found in three main locations:
  • Blood vessels: Controls vascular diameter and blood pressure
  • Gastrointestinal tract: Propels food through peristalsis
  • Other hollow organs: Bladder, uterus, airways, and pupils

II. STRUCTURE

Smooth muscle develops force through actin-myosin interactions. Myosin concentrations are much lower than in skeletal muscle, but actin levels are similar. Individual cells contain minisarcomeres - small contractile units that lack regular alignment, resulting in no visible striations.

III. EXCITATION-CONTRACTION COUPLING

Smooth muscle contraction is regulated by Ca¬≤‚Å∫-calmodulin-dependent myosin light chain kinase (MLCK). Unlike skeletal muscle where Ca¬≤‚Å∫ regulates thin filaments, smooth muscle regulation occurs on the thick filament through phosphorylation of the 20-kDa myosin light chain (MLC20).

A. Initiation

Smooth muscle contraction begins with an increase in intracellular Ca¬≤‚Å∫ concentration:
  • Ca¬≤‚Å∫ influx: Through L-type channels, ROCCs, and SOCs
  • Ca¬≤‚Å∫ release: From SR via CICR and IP‚ÇÉ pathways
  • Ca¬≤‚Å∫-Calmodulin binding: Forms Ca‚ÇÑ-CaM complex
  • MLCK activation: Ca‚ÇÑ-CaM activates myosin light chain kinase

B. Cross-bridge Cycling

The molecular mechanism of smooth muscle contraction follows these steps:
  1. MLC20 phosphorylation: MLCK phosphorylates Ser19 on MLC20
  2. Myosin activation: Phosphorylated myosin can bind actin
  3. Cross-bridge cycling: ATP-dependent cycling generates force
  4. Force maintenance: Latchbridge formation for sustained contraction

C. Relaxation

Muscle relaxation occurs when sarcoplasmic Ca¬≤‚Å∫ concentrations renormalize. Because smooth muscle contraction involves a kinase and phosphorylation step, relaxation requires a phosphatase. Unique to smooth muscle is the latchbridge state, allowing sustained contraction with minimal ATP use (~1% of skeletal muscle requirement).

D. Latchbridge Formation

Unique to smooth muscle: sustained force with minimal ATP use

Mechanism

  • Myosin dephosphorylated while attached to actin
  • Locks or "latches" in place
  • Intrinsically slow cycling rate
  • Maintains tension with minimal energy

üí° Fundamental Property: Latchbridge State

The latchbridge mechanism is a unique and fundamental property of smooth muscle that enables prolonged contractions essential for physiologic functions. When myosin is dephosphorylated while still attached to actin, it becomes "locked" in place with an intrinsically slow cycling rate. This allows blood vessels to maintain tone, sphincters to stay closed, and hollow organs to sustain pressure for hours with only ~1% of the ATP that skeletal muscle would require.

IV. CYTOSKELETAL REMODELING

In addition to the traditional contractile unit (minisarcomere), the cytoskeleton also plays a role in smooth force production. This force production is minor compared to that of the minisarcomere, but two benefits of cytoskeletal changes are adapting to changes in length and altering rigidity.

Cytoskeletal remodeling in smooth muscle showing length adaptation and isoform switching mechanisms

A. Length Adaptation

Smooth muscle can adapt to chronic changes in length by remodeling its cytoskeleton:
  • Sarcomere addition/subtraction: Adjusts optimal length
  • Dense body repositioning: Reorganizes contractile apparatus
  • Intermediate filament remodeling: Maintains structural integrity
  • Time course: Days to weeks for complete adaptation

B. Alternate Actin and Myosin Isoforms

Different smooth muscle types express distinct protein isoforms:
  • Œ±-smooth muscle actin: Vascular and visceral smooth muscle
  • Œ≥-smooth muscle actin: Primarily in GI tract
  • Myosin heavy chain isoforms: SM1 and SM2 variants
  • Functional consequences: Different contractile velocities and Ca¬≤‚Å∫ sensitivity

V. TYPES

Smooth muscle is a diverse tissue type that can be difficult to classify. One of the more helpful classifications is based on function. Phasic smooth muscle contracts transiently when stimulated, while tonic smooth muscle is capable of sustained contractions.

Comparison of phasic (single unit) and tonic (multi unit) smooth muscle types showing structural and functional differences

Phasic Smooth Muscle

  • Location: GI tract, urogenital tract
  • Function: Transient contractions
  • Structure: Single unit (unitary) smooth muscle
  • Coupling: Extensive gap junctions
  • Contraction speed: Faster than tonic
  • Examples: Stomach, intestines, bladder, uterus

Tonic Smooth Muscle

  • Location: Blood vessels, sphincters, airways
  • Function: Sustained contractions
  • Structure: Multi-unit smooth muscle
  • Coupling: Limited gap junctions
  • Contraction speed: Slower, more sustained
  • Examples: Arteries, iris muscles, ciliary muscles

üí° Functional Significance

Most organs contain a blend of phasic and tonic smooth muscle tailored to specific functional needs. This allows for both rapid, coordinated responses (phasic) and sustained regulatory control (tonic), providing the versatility needed for complex physiological processes.
